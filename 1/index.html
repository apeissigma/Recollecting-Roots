Trophies of Exploitation

This code sketch utilizes 14 Angkorian artifacts—heads of gods, bodhisattvas, dieties, and royalty—found in the collections of the Art Institute of Chicago, Minneapolis Institute of Art, Metropolitan Museum of Art, and the Cleveland Museum of Art. While I knew that artifacts like these are almost always illegally obtained before they are “generously donated” to Western institutions, it wasn't until my trip to Cambodia that I got a bit more context. My mother's cousin Sopheap, a trained guide under the APSARA National Authority, had explained to me the nature of the antiques market as we looked upon a corridor of headless statues in the main enclosure of the Angkor Wat. European foreigners, self-proclaimed collectors, were more than willing to pay a hefty price to put these heads in their display cabinets to collect dust a million miles away. For an impoverished country milked dry by the hands of colonialism, the decapitation of bygone gods and ancestors was a means of survival.

This code displays these heads at random coordinates across the canvas, building and overlapping each other into an indistinguishable mass of stone. These heads, which once belonged to a holy body and were worshiped as such, are now displaced, scattered across the globe in foreign lands for onlookers to gawk at (for the price of admission, of course). The bases of their necks are crude and uneven, a testament to the brutality with which they were ripped from their bodies by blunt blades. To me, these heads are an unfortunate byproduct of colonization and degradation—the legacy of a bodhisattva or king is reduced to a fetish of Orientalism to collect and sell.
